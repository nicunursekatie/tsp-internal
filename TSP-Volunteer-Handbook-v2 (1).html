Temperature Limits

So what counts as ‚Äúcold‚Äù? Here are the numbers to know:

39¬∞F (4¬∞C)

Maximum safe temperature. Above this, bacteria begin to grow.

80¬∞F (27¬∞C)

Danger zone. If deli meat reaches this temperature, it must be discarded.

34¬∞‚Äì38¬∞F is the target range for any refrigerator storing TSP sandwiches or ingredients.

The Two-Hour Rule

Every time food breaks the cold chain‚Äîevery moment it‚Äôs above 39¬∞F‚Äîbacteria are growing. The two-hour rule puts a hard limit on how much of that we can allow:

‚ö† The Two-Hour Rule

Deli meat must stay at or below 39¬∞F (refrigerator temperature) at all times, except for a cumulative total of two hours across the entire process‚Äîfrom factory to consumer. This is based on USDA, FDA, and CDC guidelines.

This is not ‚Äútwo hours at a time.‚Äù The clock runs across every step from purchase to delivery. And once bacteria start growing, re-cooling doesn‚Äôt reverse it‚Äîthat‚Äôs why every minute counts.

Two hours sounds like plenty‚Äîuntil you add up all the little moments the food is above 39¬∞F:

  • Shopping: Pick up deli ingredients last.
  • Sandwich-making: Take out only one package of meat and cheese at a time.
  • Transfers: Move quickly between fridge, cooler, and car. Keep doors and lids closed as much as possible.
  • Transport: Drive directly to your destination, no stops.

Food Safety Foundations

Refrigeration

Your refrigerator is the workhorse of the cold chain. It‚Äôs the only piece of equipment that actively lowers and maintains temperature. Here‚Äôs how to make sure it‚Äôs doing its job:

  • Maintain 34¬∞‚Äì38¬∞F. Check the temperature regularly‚Äîdon‚Äôt just assume it‚Äôs right.
  • Don‚Äôt overfill. This is one of the most common mistakes. An overpacked fridge can‚Äôt circulate air properly, which creates warm spots‚Äîeven if the thermometer reads fine. Leave room around food for airflow.
  • Minimize door openings. Temperatures rise fast when the door is open, especially in hot weather. Open briefly, get what you need, close it.
  • Keep organized. Store PB&J and deli sandwiches separately. Label everything. Remove personal food items to maximize TSP space.
  • Freezer shortcut for hot weather. On hot days, you can put sandwiches in the freezer for 15‚Äì20 minutes before loading coolers to give them extra chill. Don‚Äôt exceed 20 minutes.

Coolers Are Not Refrigerators

Key Concept

Coolers keep cold food cold. They don‚Äôt cool warm food down. Unlike a refrigerator, a cooler doesn‚Äôt generate cold air‚Äîit just holds in whatever temperature the food already is.

  • Don‚Äôt put warm sandwiches into a cooler‚Äîthey need to be fridge-cold first.
  • A properly packed cooler can hold safe temps for up to one hour during active transport.
  • If a cooler has been sitting in the heat, bring it inside to cool completely before packing.

Maximizing Cooler Performance

  • Pack quickly and in the shade. Keep the cooler closed between trips to the fridge.
  • Ice packs everywhere. Layer them between loaves and on top. Every loaf should be in direct contact with an ice pack.
  • Fill it completely. Tightly packed coolers with minimal air space stay colder significantly longer than half-full ones.
  • Keep the lid closed. Every opening lets warm air in. Do not open coolers during transport.

‚ö† No Coolers in the Trunk

Always keep coolers in the vehicle cabin where A/C circulates. Trunks lack airflow and heat up fast‚Äîeven on mild days. Packed coolers should never be left in unattended vehicles.

Hygiene Standards

Food safety isn‚Äôt just about temperature‚Äîit‚Äôs also about clean hands, clean surfaces, and clean equipment.

Personal Hygiene

  • Tie back hair longer than chin-length (including bangs), or use a hairnet. Use a beard net for facial hair. Do this before washing your hands.
  • Wash hands with soap and water before starting any food handling, during prep as needed, and when you‚Äôre finished.
  • Hand sanitizer is not a substitute for handwashing. Alcohol residue can transfer to gloves and food, and sanitizer doesn‚Äôt remove all bacteria, food residue, or contaminants. We know it‚Äôs more convenient, but soap and water is the way to go.
  • Wear food-safe gloves after washing hands. Change gloves any time you touch something other than food ingredients.

Clean Spaces

  • Work surfaces: Clean counters and wash utensils before starting. A disposable tablecloth makes prep and cleanup easy.
  • Coolers: Wash with soap and warm water after each use. Air-dry completely before storing and covering tightly. Use only dedicated TSP coolers for sandwiches‚Äînever for raw meat, fish, or other items.
  • Refrigerators: Clean regularly. Keep personal items in a separate fridge.
  • Collection areas: Keep clean, uncluttered, and free of strong odors. Store all food, coolers, and ice packs off the floor and away from pets, trash, chemicals, and tools.

When in Doubt, Throw It Out

We know it‚Äôs hard to toss food‚Äîespecially food you bought and prepared yourself. But food can make people sick even when it looks, smells, and tastes fine. If something doesn‚Äôt seem right, trust your instincts and discard it. It‚Äôs always better to be safe.

Common Questions

Q: How do I keep sandwiches safe during prep?

A: Get deli meat back in the fridge as quickly as possible. Only take out one package of meat and one package of cheese at a time. Make one loaf, refrigerate it, start the next.

Q: What is the maximum safe temperature for deli meat?

A: Deli meat must never reach 80¬∞F (27¬∞C). The target is always below 39¬∞F (4¬∞C).

Q: What does the two-hour rule actually mean?

A: Deli meat and cheese can only be above 39¬∞F for two hours total, cumulatively, across the entire journey from factory to consumer. That‚Äôs not two hours at a time‚Äîit‚Äôs two hours total. And once bacteria start growing, cooling the food back down doesn‚Äôt undo it.

Q: Can I re-cool sandwiches that got warm?

A: No. Re-cooling does not kill bacteria that have already grown. If sandwiches have been above 39¬∞F for more than two cumulative hours‚Äîor if you‚Äôre not sure‚Äîdiscard them.

For Sandwich Makers

You‚Äôre the first link in the cold chain‚Äîand for many of our recipients, you‚Äôre the reason they‚Äôll have a meal today. The care you put into shopping, prepping, and packing makes a real difference.

Shopping

  • Buy the same quality ingredients you‚Äôd feed your own family.
  • Choose the furthest expiration dates available‚Äîat least 7 days past your drop-off date.
  • Bring a cooler with ice packs. Park in the shade. Pick up deli items last‚Äîthe two-hour clock starts the moment they leave the store‚Äôs refrigerated case. Go directly home, no stops.
  • Refrigerate meat and cheese immediately when you get home.

  • Don‚Äôt overpack the fridge‚Äîair needs to circulate to keep food properly chilled.

Preparation

  • Clean and cover counters (a disposable tablecloth works well).
  • Tie back hair (including bangs) or use a hairnet. Use beard nets for facial hair.
  • Wash hands with soap and water before putting on food-safe gloves. Change gloves any time you touch something other than food.
  • Keep peanut butter and nut/shellfish products away from deli ingredients. Use separate knives for jelly and peanut butter.

Making PB&J Sandwiches

  • Lay out two slices of bread.
  • Using a clean knife, spread 1 tablespoon of peanut butter on one slice and 2 tablespoons on the other (‚âà half a ping pong ball per tablespoon). Spread evenly, leaving a clean border around the bread.
  • With a separate knife, spread 2 teaspoons of jelly (‚âà two grapes) on top of the peanut butter on one slice. Use jelly only‚Äîno jam.
  • Press slices together neatly. No peanut butter or jelly should ooze to the edges.
  • Place the sandwich in an individual sandwich-size bag. Remove excess air and seal.
  • Return the sealed bag to its original loaf bag. Keep the twist tie‚Äîyou‚Äôll use it to close the loaf bag when it‚Äôs full.

Making Deli Sandwiches

  • Take out one package of meat and one package of cheese at a time. Keep the rest in the fridge‚Äîdeli ingredients are on the two-hour clock the moment they leave the refrigerator.
  • Lay out two slices of bread.
  • Place 1 slice of cheese on each side (2 slices total).
  • Add at least 2 ounces of meat on one side (check the serving size on your packaging).
  • No condiments‚Äîno lettuce, tomato, mayo, or mustard. Just bread, meat, and cheese.
  • Press slices together neatly.
  • Bag each sandwich individually, remove excess air, seal, and return to the loaf bag.

Keeping It Cold While You Work

This is where the two-hour rule matters most. Every minute meat spends outside the fridge counts toward that cumulative limit.

  • Make one loaf at a time. Immediately return each finished loaf to the fridge before starting the next.
  • Only have one package of meat and one package of cheese out at a time.
  • Working with others? Divide tasks: some assemble while others bag, refrigerate finished loaves, and retrieve ingredients.

Packaging

Don‚Äôt discard twist ties or the bags from your loaves of bread‚Äîyou‚Äôll need them. Finished sandwiches go into individual bags, then back into the loaf bag, secured with the original twist tie. Don‚Äôt tie or tape bags shut. Consider purchasing extra twist ties if you make sandwiches regularly.

Timing

Please don‚Äôt make sandwiches before Tuesday evening. They need to reach a host home within 24 hours of preparation to stay safe.

  • Use the correct label: Deli or PB&J.
  • Fill out all fields, including the date made.
  • Label dates should be no earlier than Tuesday evening (Wednesday preferred).

Common Questions

Q: Should I buy pre-packaged or deli-sliced meat?

A: Pre-packaged only. Prepackaged deli meat is processed and sealed in controlled environments with consistent food safety standards. Counter-sliced meat introduces variables we can‚Äôt control.

Q: How long can I keep opened ingredients?

A: Opened meat: 3 days. All meat/cheese: 2 weeks max from purchase, regardless of packaging or expiration date.

A: Trunks lack airflow and heat up fast‚Äîeven on mild days. A/C only circulates in the cabin.

Q: How long can a cooler keep things cold?
